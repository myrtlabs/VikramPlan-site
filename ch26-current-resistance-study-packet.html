1. Key Concepts

1.1 Electric Current

Current is the rate of charge flow through a cross-section of a conductor.

$$i = \frac{dq}{dt}$$
  • Conventional current direction: Flow of positive charge (opposite to electron flow)
  • Units: Amperes (A = C/s)
  • Key principle: Current through a wire cross-section equals all charge carriers passing through per unit time
Critical fact: Current is the SAME everywhere in a series circuit. This follows from charge conservation — no charge accumulates at any point.

1.2 Current Density

Current density describes current per unit cross-sectional area. It's a more local quantity than total current.

$$J = \frac{i}{A} \quad \text{(uniform)} \quad \text{or} \quad i = \int \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{A}$$
  • J is a vector: Points in direction of current flow
  • Relationship to drift velocity: $\vec{J} = ne\vec{v}_d$ where n = carrier density, e = electron charge, v_d = drift speed
  • Units: A/m²
Key insight: Drift velocity is TINY (mm/s) compared to signal speed (≈ c). The electric field propagates near the speed of light, but individual electrons drift slowly. This seems paradoxical but is fundamental to current flow.

1.3 Resistance and Resistivity

Resistance depends on both the material (resistivity) and the geometry of the conductor.

$$R = \frac{V}{I} \quad \text{(definition, units: ohms, Ω = V/A)}$$ $$\rho = \frac{E}{J} \quad \text{(resistivity, units: Ω·m)}$$ $$\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho} \quad \text{(conductivity, units: S/m)}$$ $$R = \rho \frac{L}{A} \quad \text{(geometry: L = length, A = cross-section area)}$$
  • Resistance R: Property of a specific object. Depends on material AND geometry.
  • Resistivity ρ: Property of a material only. Does not depend on size or shape.
Common mistake: Confusing resistance (specific object) with resistivity (material property). Remember: you can measure R across a resistor, but ρ is an intrinsic property you look up in a table.

1.4 Temperature Dependence of Resistivity

For most materials, resistivity changes with temperature in a predictable way.

$$\rho = \rho_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$$
  • α = temperature coefficient of resistivity: Units K⁻¹ or °C⁻¹
  • For metals: α > 0 (resistance increases with temperature, like tungsten filament)
  • For semiconductors: α < 0 (resistance decreases with temperature — more carriers become available)
  • Note: This linear model only works over a limited temperature range
Why it matters: A lightbulb filament is hot (thousands of K), so its resistance when operating is much higher than its room-temperature resistance. This is why hot filaments draw less current than cold filaments at the same voltage.

1.5 Ohm's Law — What It Really Means

Ohm's law is often misunderstood. The equation V = IR is a definition of resistance, NOT Ohm's law.

Definition: $$R = \frac{V}{I}$$

Ohm's law: For many materials, R is constant (independent of V and I) over a wide range.
  • Ohmic materials: Metals at constant temperature follow Ohm's law (linear I-V curve)
  • Non-ohmic devices: Diodes, transistors, gas-discharge tubes do NOT follow Ohm's law (curved I-V)
Important: Ohm's law is an empirical observation, not a fundamental law like Gauss's law. It happens to work very well for metals but is not universal.

1.6 Power in Electric Circuits

Power is the rate of energy transfer or dissipation in an electrical device.

$$P = IV \quad \text{(general form)}$$ $$P = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \quad \text{(for resistors, using V = IR)}$$
  • Units: Watts (W = J/s)
  • Interpretation: Power dissipated in a resistor is converted to heat
Strategy: Use $P = I^2R$ when current is known or constant. Use $P = V^2/R$ when voltage is known or constant. Both give the same answer for a given resistor, but one may be more convenient depending on what you know.

1.7 Common Mistakes on Tests

  • Confusing R and ρ: Resistance is for an object; resistivity is for a material. Use $R = \rho L/A$ to convert.
  • Area calculation errors: Always use cross-sectional area (perpendicular to current flow), not surface area. For a wire: $A = \pi r^2$ (radius, not diameter).
  • Diameter vs. radius: If given diameter d, convert: $r = d/2$ before calculating $A = \pi r^2$.
  • Temperature effects: Don't forget to account for resistance change with temperature in multi-part problems.
  • Confusing J and i: Current density J is per unit area; total current i is the sum over all areas. $i = JA$ only for uniform J.
  • Wire stretching: When a wire is drawn to n× its length, volume stays constant, so area → 1/n. Resistance becomes n²R₀ (both L and 1/A contribute a factor of n).

Key Relationships to Memorize

  • R depends on material AND geometry: $R = \rho L/A$. Resistivity depends on material only.
  • Wire stretched to n× length: New resistance = n²R₀ (because L increases by n and A decreases by n, giving n × n = n²).
  • Same current flows through series components: $i_1 = i_2 = i_3$ (charge conservation).
  • Current density varies with area: $J_1A_1 = J_2A_2$ (same current, different areas).
  • Electric field inside a resistor: $E = \rho J = V/L$ (where V is voltage across the resistor).

Tier 3 Solutions

11. Non-uniform Current Density Derivation: (a) Total current: $$i = \int_0^R J_0(r/R)^2(2\pi r \, dr) = \frac{2\pi J_0}{R^2} \int_0^R r^3 \, dr = \frac{2\pi J_0}{R^2} \cdot \frac{R^4}{4} = \frac{\pi J_0 R^2}{2}$$ (b) Average current density: $$J_{\text{avg}} = \frac{i}{A} = \frac{(\pi J_0 R^2/2)}{\pi R^2} = \frac{J_0}{2}$$ (c) Uniform $J_0$ everywhere: $i_{\text{uniform}} = J_0 \pi R^2$, which is twice the non-uniform case. The surface-weighted quadratic distribution concentrates current in the center, reducing total current.
12. Wire Drawing Analysis: (a) After n passes: $L_n = L_0 \cdot 2^n$ and $A_n = A_0/2^n$. Thus: $$R_n = \rho \frac{L_n}{A_n} = \rho \frac{L_0 \cdot 2^n}{A_0/2^n} = R_0 \cdot 4^n$$ (b) For 1000× increase: $4^n = 1000$; $n = \log_4(1000) = \ln(1000)/\ln(4) \approx 4.98 \approx 5$ passes (c) Current density changes dramatically: $J = V/(RA) = V/(R_0 \cdot 4^n \cdot A_0/2^n)$ decreases with each pass.
13. Power Line Transmission: (a) $A = \pi(0.005)^2 = 7.854 \times 10^{-5}$ m². $R = (1.69 \times 10^{-8})(100,000)/(7.854 \times 10^{-5}) = 21.5$ Ω (b) At 10 kV: $I = 500,000/10,000 = 50$ A. $P_{\text{loss}} = I^2R = (50)^2(21.5) = 53.75$ kW (c) At 100 kV: $I = 500,000/100,000 = 5$ A. $P_{\text{loss}} = (5)^2(21.5) = 537.5$ W (d) At 10 kV: $53,750/500,000 = 10.8\%$ loss. At 100 kV: $537.5/500,000 = 0.11\%$ loss (e) Higher transmission voltage → same power at lower current → dramatically reduced $I^2R$ losses. Power companies use high voltage (10s to 100s of kV) to minimize transmission losses over long distances.
14. Non-uniform Resistivity: $$R = \int_0^L \frac{\rho_0(1+x/L)}{A} dx = \frac{\rho_0}{A} \int_0^L (1+x/L) \, dx = \frac{\rho_0}{A} \left[x + \frac{x^2}{2L}\right]_0^L$$ $$R = \frac{\rho_0}{A} \left(L + \frac{L}{2}\right) = \frac{3\rho_0 L}{2A}$$ Equivalent uniform resistivity: $\rho_{\text{eff}} = R \cdot A/L = 3\rho_0/2$ (average of minimum ρ₀ and maximum 2ρ₀).
15. Complete Circuit Analysis: (a) $A = \pi(0.4 \times 10^{-3})^2 = 5.027 \times 10^{-7}$ m². $R_{\text{wire}} = (1.69 \times 10^{-8})(20)/(5.027 \times 10^{-7}) = 0.672$ Ω (b) $I = 12/(0.672+0.50) = 10.24$ A (c) $E = V_{\text{wire}}/L = IR_{\text{wire}}/L = (10.24)(0.672)/20 = 0.344$ V/m (d) $v_d = J/(ne) = (I/A)/(ne) = (10.24/(5.027 \times 10^{-7}))/[(8.49 \times 10^{28})(1.6 \times 10^{-19})] = 1.50 \times 10^{-3}$ m/s (e) $P_{\text{wire}} = I^2R_{\text{wire}} = (10.24)^2(0.672) = 70.5$ W. $P_{\text{internal}} = I^2r = (10.24)^2(0.50) = 52.4$ W. Wire dissipates more than internal resistance. (f) $t = L/v_d = 20/(1.50 \times 10^{-3}) = 13,300$ s ≈ 3.7 hours
