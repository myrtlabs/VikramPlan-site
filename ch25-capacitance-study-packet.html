1. Key Concepts

Capacitance ‚Äî The Basics

A capacitor stores charge by having two conductors separated by an insulator (dielectric). The fundamental definition is:

\(C = \frac{q}{V}\)

where \(q\) is the magnitude of charge on either plate and \(V\) is the potential difference between them. Units: Farads (F), where \(1 \text{ F} = 1 \text{ C/V}\).

Key Point: Capacitance \(C\) depends ONLY on the geometry (plate area, separation, shape) and the dielectric material between the plates. It does NOT depend on \(q\) or \(V\). Capacitance is always positive.

Calculating Capacitance (Three Geometries)

Capacitance formulas are derived using Gauss's Law to find the electric field, then integrating to find the potential difference.

Parallel-Plate Capacitor: \(C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}\)
where \(A\) = plate area, \(d\) = plate separation
Cylindrical Capacitor: \(C = \frac{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L}{\ln(b/a)}\)
where \(a\) = inner radius, \(b\) = outer radius, \(L\) = length
Spherical Capacitor: \(C = \frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0 ab}{b - a}\)
where \(a\) = inner radius, \(b\) = outer radius
Isolated Sphere: \(C = 4\pi\varepsilon_0 R\)
(sphere of radius \(R\) in free space, as \(b \to \infty\))
Key Point: These formulas are derived from first principles using Gauss's law. You should be able to re-derive at least the parallel-plate case from scratch on the AP exam.

Capacitors in Parallel and Series

Parallel Connection: Both capacitors connected across the same two nodes ‚Üí same voltage \(V\) across each. Charges add.

\(C_{\text{eq}} = C_1 + C_2 + C_3 + \cdots\)

Series Connection: Capacitors connected one after the other in a single loop ‚Üí same charge \(q\) on each. Voltages add.

\(\frac{1}{C_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} + \cdots\)

For two capacitors in series:

\(C_{\text{eq}} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2}\)
TRAP ALERT: Capacitors behave OPPOSITE to resistors. Capacitors in parallel ‚Üí MORE capacitance. Capacitors in series ‚Üí LESS capacitance. Don't confuse them!

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

A capacitor stores electrical energy in the electric field between its plates. There are three equivalent forms:

\(U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}\frac{q^2}{C} = \frac{1}{2}qV\)

The energy density (energy per unit volume) in the field is:

\(u = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2\)
Critical Distinction: When a capacitor is disconnected from the battery (charge fixed), inserting a dielectric DECREASES stored energy ‚Äî the electric field does positive work pulling the dielectric in. When the battery stays connected (voltage fixed), inserting a dielectric INCREASES stored energy ‚Äî the battery does work pushing more charge onto the plates.

Dielectrics

A dielectric material between the plates increases the capacitance by a factor called the dielectric constant, \(\kappa\) (kappa):

\(C = \kappa C_{\text{air}}\)

For a parallel-plate capacitor with dielectric:

\(C = \frac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d}\)

The dielectric material weakens the internal electric field by its polarization:

\(E = \frac{E_0}{\kappa}\)

where \(E_0\) is the field in vacuum.

Why Dielectrics Help: (1) They increase \(C\), allowing more charge storage at the same voltage. (2) They increase the dielectric strength (max field before breakdown), so you can apply higher voltages without arcing.

Common Mistakes on Tests

  • Forgetting that \(C_{\text{series}} <\) smallest individual \(C\) ‚Äî the opposite of resistors.
  • Confusing "battery connected" vs "battery disconnected" scenarios. Connected ‚Üí \(V\) is fixed, \(q\) changes. Disconnected ‚Üí \(q\) is fixed, \(V\) changes.
  • Not using energy density \(u = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2\) when asked about field energy or when finding total energy from \(U = u \times \text{volume}\).
  • Sign/direction errors with multiple dielectrics or partial dielectrics (e.g., one filling half the gap).
  • Unit conversion mistakes: ŒºF, pF, mm, cm, m. Always convert to SI before calculating.
  • Forgetting that the capacitance of a sphere is not \(4\pi\varepsilon_0 R\) unless it's isolated (infinite outer radius).

Key Relationships:

  • Parallel: Same \(V\), charges add \(\Rightarrow C_{\text{eq}} = C_1 + C_2 + \cdots\)
  • Series: Same \(q\), voltages add \(\Rightarrow 1/C_{\text{eq}} = 1/C_1 + 1/C_2 + \cdots\)
  • Opposite of resistors: More \(C\) in parallel, less \(C\) in series.

Q1. Which plot (in Fig. 25-19) goes with which capacitor (in the table)?

Match each capacitor's capacitance value to the plot with the corresponding slope. The capacitor with the largest \(A/d\) ratio has the steepest plot; the smallest \(A/d\) has the shallowest plot.
Q5. (a) Are \(C_1\) and \(C_3\) in series in Fig. 25-22a? (b) Are \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) in parallel? (c) Rank the equivalent capacitances.
(a) Series means a single path: all charge must flow through both. Check if they're on the same branch without intermediate nodes. Examine Fig. 25-22a carefully.
(b) Parallel means both are connected to the same two nodes, ensuring the same voltage across each. Check if \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) share both nodes.
(c) Ranking depends on the specific circuit topology shown in Fig 25-22. Identify series/parallel groups in all four circuits and calculate or compare \(C_{\text{eq}}\) for each.
Q9. In Fig. 25-23, after switches close, in which circuit (if any) will the charge on the left-hand capacitor (a) increase, (b) decrease, (c) remain the same?
When a charged capacitor is connected to uncharged capacitor(s), charge redistributes according to: \(q_{\text{total}} = q_{\text{initial}}\) (charge conservation).
The voltage on each capacitor after connection is \(V = q/C\). Compare the initial voltage on the left capacitor to the final voltage to determine if its charge increased or decreased.
Examine Fig. 25-23 for all three circuits and identify which, if any, fit each category (increase, decrease, or no change).
Q11. When a dielectric is inserted into one of two identical capacitors in Fig. 25-24, what happens to (a) capacitance, (b) charge, (c) potential difference, (d) potential energy of that capacitor, and (e) the same properties of the other capacitor?
The answer depends on whether the capacitors are in series or parallel with a battery, or isolated. Examine Fig. 25-24 carefully.
If in series with a battery (battery connected): voltage is fixed across the series combination. Dielectric increases one \(C\). Since \(V_{\text{total}}\) is fixed and this cap's \(C\) increases, more charge flows onto it and its \(V\) decreases. The other cap must then have increased \(V\) and possibly changed charge.
If isolated: total charge is fixed. Dielectric increases one \(C\). Since \(q\) is conserved and \(C\) increases, that cap's voltage decreases. Charge redistributes between them.
If in parallel: voltage across both is same. Dielectric increases one \(C\), allowing more charge on it while the other stays at same voltage.

\((100 + C_2)(35) = 5.0 \times 10^{-9}\) (in pF and V)
\(100 + C_2 = \frac{5.0 \times 10^{-9}}{35} \times 10^{12} = 142.86\) pF
\(\mathbf{C_2 = 43 \text{ pF}}\)
P21. 12.0 V battery charges \(C_1 = 4.00\) ŒºF to full charge. Then switch thrown right: \(C_1\) parallel with \(C_2 = 6.00\) ŒºF and \(C_3 = 3.00\) ŒºF (in series). Find final charge on (a) \(C_1\), (b) \(C_2\), (c) \(C_3\).

\(q_1 = C_1(12.0) = (4.00 \times 10^{-6})(12.0) = 48.0 \times 10^{-6}\) C = 48.0 ŒºC
Step 2: \(C_2\) and \(C_3\) in series.
\(C_{23} = \frac{C_2 C_3}{C_2 + C_3} = \frac{(6.00)(3.00)}{9.00} = 2.00\) ŒºF
Step 3: \(C_1\) (charged to 48.0 ŒºC) connects in parallel to \(C_{23}\) (uncharged).
Total charge: \(q_{\text{total}} = 48.0\) ŒºC (conserved)
\(V_f = \frac{q_{\text{total}}}{C_1 + C_{23}} = \frac{48.0 \times 10^{-6}}{4.00 + 2.00} = 8.0\) V
(a) \(q_{1,f} = C_1 V_f = (4.00)(8.0) = \mathbf{32.0 \text{ ŒºC}}\)
(b) \(q_{23} = C_{23} V_f = (2.00)(8.0) = 16.0\) ŒºC
(c) Since \(C_2\) and \(C_3\) are in series, same charge on both:
\(\mathbf{q_2 = q_3 = 16.0 \text{ ŒºC}}\)
P27. \(C_1 = 2.0\) ŒºF and \(C_2 = 4.0\) ŒºF in parallel across \(V = 300\) V. Calculate total energy stored.
Parallel ‚Üí same \(V\) across each.
Total \(C = C_1 + C_2 = 2.0 + 4.0 = 6.0\) ŒºF
\(U_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}C_{\text{total}}V^2 = \frac{1}{2}(6.0 \times 10^{-6})(300)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(6.0 \times 10^{-6})(9.0 \times 10^4) = \mathbf{0.27 \text{ J}}\)
P31. \(A = 8.50\) cm¬≤, \(d_0 = 3.00\) mm, \(V_0 = 6.00\) V. Battery disconnects. Plates pulled to \(d_f = 8.00\) mm. Find (a) final \(V\), (b) initial \(U\), (c) final \(U\), (d) work required.
Initial capacitance: \(C_0 = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d_0} = \frac{(8.854 \times 10^{-12})(8.50 \times 10^{-4})}{3.00 \times 10^{-3}} = 2.508\) pF
Charge (fixed, battery disconnected): \(q = C_0 V_0 = (2.508 \times 10^{-12})(6.00) = 15.05 \times 10^{-12}\) C
Final capacitance: \(C_f = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d_f} = \frac{(8.854 \times 10^{-12})(8.50 \times 10^{-4})}{8.00 \times 10^{-3}} = 0.9407\) pF
(a) Final voltage: \(V_f = \frac{q}{C_f} = \frac{15.05 \times 10^{-12}}{0.9407 \times 10^{-12}} = \mathbf{16.0 \text{ V}}\)
(b) Initial energy: \(U_0 = \frac{1}{2}q V_0 = \frac{1}{2}(15.05 \times 10^{-12})(6.00) = \mathbf{45.1 \text{ pJ}}\)
(c) Final energy: \(U_f = \frac{1}{2}q V_f = \frac{1}{2}(15.05 \times 10^{-12})(16.0) = \mathbf{120 \text{ pJ}}\)
(d) Work done (external agent): \(W = U_f - U_0 = 120 - 45.1 = \mathbf{75.2 \text{ pJ}}\)
(You did work pulling the plates apart against the attractive electric force.)
P35. \(C_{\text{air}} = 7.4\) pF. Must store \(U = 7.4\) ŒºJ at \(V_{\text{max}} = 652\) V (no margin). Which dielectric from Table 25-1?

11. Derive cylindrical capacitor: \(C = \frac{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L}{\ln(b/a)}\).
Setup: Inner cylinder radius \(a\), outer radius \(b\), length \(L\), with charge \(+q\) on inner, \(-q\) on outer.
Gaussian surface: Cylinder of radius \(r\) (where \(a < r < b\)), coaxial with the capacitor, length \(L\).
Gauss's law: \(\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}\)
By symmetry, \(E\) is radial. Flux through curved surface: \(E(2\pi r L) = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}\)
\(E(r) = \frac{q}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L r}\)
Potential difference: \(V = -\int_b^a E(r) dr = \int_a^b \frac{q}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L r} dr = \frac{q}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L} \ln(b/a)\)
Capacitance: \(C = \frac{q}{V} = \frac{q}{\frac{q}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L}\ln(b/a)} = \mathbf{\frac{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L}{\ln(b/a)}}\)

15. Partial dielectric filling: parallel-plate (\(A\), \(d\)), dielectric slab (\(\kappa\), thickness \(t\) where \(t < d\)). Battery \(V_0\) connected.
Setup: Air gap of thickness \(d - t\), dielectric of thickness \(t\), in series.
(a) Electric fields:
Let \(\sigma\) = surface charge density on plates. By Gauss's law (conductor/dielectric boundary):
\(E_{\text{air}} = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}\), \(E_{\text{diel}} = \frac{\sigma}{\kappa\varepsilon_0}\)
(b) Surface charge density on plates:
Voltage constraint: \(E_{\text{air}}(d-t) + E_{\text{diel}} \cdot t = V_0\)
\(\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}(d-t) + \frac{\sigma}{\kappa\varepsilon_0}t = V_0\)
\(\sigma\left[\frac{d-t}{\varepsilon_0} + \frac{t}{\kappa\varepsilon_0}\right] = V_0\)
\(\sigma = \frac{\varepsilon_0 V_0}{d - t + t/\kappa}\)
(c) Induced surface charge on dielectric:
Polarization reduces field by factor \(\kappa\). Bound surface charge density:
\(\sigma_{\text{bound}} = \sigma\left(1 - \frac{1}{\kappa}\right) = \frac{\varepsilon_0 V_0}{d - t + t/\kappa}\left(1 - \frac{1}{\kappa}\right)\)
(d) Total capacitance:
\(q = \sigma A\). \(C = \frac{q}{V_0} = \frac{\sigma A}{V_0} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d - t + t/\kappa}\)
(e) Check limits:
If \(t = 0\) (no dielectric): \(C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}\) ‚úì
If \(t = d\) (full dielectric): \(C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d - d + d/\kappa} = \frac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d}\) ‚úì
