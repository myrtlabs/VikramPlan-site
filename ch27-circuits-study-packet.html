Key Concepts

1. EMF, Internal Resistance & Terminal Voltage

A real battery has an internal resistance \(r\). The EMF (\(\mathcal{E}\)) is the ideal voltage with no current flowing. When current \(I\) flows:

$$V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} - Ir \quad \text{(discharging / normal operation)}$$

The voltage you measure at the terminals is less than the EMF. The "lost" voltage \(Ir\) is dissipated as heat inside the battery.

Trap: When a battery is being charged (current forced backwards), \(V_{\text{terminal}} = \mathcal{E} + Ir\) ‚Äî the terminal voltage is higher than the EMF.

2. Kirchhoff's Rules

Junction Rule (Conservation of Charge):

$$\sum I_{\text{in}} = \sum I_{\text{out}}$$

Charge doesn't pile up at a junction.

Loop Rule (Conservation of Energy):

$$\sum \Delta V = 0 \quad \text{around any closed loop}$$

Sign conventions when traversing a loop:

Battery: \(- \to +\) = \(+\mathcal{E}\) (gain). \(+ \to -\) = \(-\mathcal{E}\) (drop).
Resistor: Against current = \(+IR\). With current = \(-IR\).

Strategy: (1) Assign current variables and directions (guess is fine ‚Äî if wrong, you get a negative number). (2) Write junction equations. (3) Write loop equations for independent loops. (4) Solve. You need as many independent equations as unknowns.

3. Resistors in Series & Parallel

Series: Same current through each. Voltages add.

$$R_{\text{eq}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \cdots$$

Parallel: Same voltage across each. Currents add.

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots \qquad \text{Two resistors: } R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$
Resistors: series = more \(R\), parallel = less \(R\). Opposite of capacitors: series = less \(C\), parallel = more \(C\).

4. Power in Circuits

Power dissipated in a resistor (three equivalent forms):

$$P = IV = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$

Power delivered by a battery:

$$P_{\text{battery}} = \mathcal{E} \cdot I$$

If current flows \(- \to +\) through the battery (normal), it delivers power. If forced \(+ \to -\) (being charged), it absorbs power.

Energy conservation check: Total power delivered by all sources = Total power dissipated in all resistors + internal resistances. Use this to verify your answers.

5. RC Circuits

When a resistor and capacitor are in a circuit together, quantities change exponentially with time constant:

$$\tau = RC \quad \text{(units: seconds ‚Äî verify: } \Omega \cdot F = \frac{V}{A} \cdot \frac{C}{V} = \frac{C}{A} = s\text{)}$$

Charging (from \(\mathcal{E}\) through \(R\) into \(C\)):

$$q(t) = C\mathcal{E}\left(1 - e^{-t/RC}\right) \qquad I(t) = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R}\,e^{-t/RC} \qquad V_C(t) = \mathcal{E}\left(1 - e^{-t/RC}\right)$$

At \(t = 0\): \(q = 0\), \(I = \mathcal{E}/R\) (max), \(V_C = 0\).
At \(t = \tau\): \(q = 0.632\,C\mathcal{E}\), \(I = 0.368\,\mathcal{E}/R\), \(V_C = 0.632\,\mathcal{E}\).
At \(t \to \infty\): \(q = C\mathcal{E}\), \(I = 0\), \(V_C = \mathcal{E}\).

Discharging (\(C\) through \(R\), no battery):

$$q(t) = Q_0\,e^{-t/RC} \qquad I(t) = -\frac{Q_0}{RC}\,e^{-t/RC} \qquad V_C(t) = V_0\,e^{-t/RC}$$

Everything decays exponentially. At each \(\tau\), the remaining quantity drops to 36.8% of its previous value.

The derivation of these equations (Kirchhoff ‚Üí separate variables ‚Üí integrate) is a classic AP free response. You MUST be able to do it from scratch.

6. Common Mistakes on Tests

  1. Sign errors in Kirchhoff's loops. Be consistent. Draw the circuit, label currents, stick with your loop direction.
  2. Confusing series/parallel for resistors vs capacitors. They're opposite.
  3. Forgetting internal resistance. \(V_{\text{terminal}} \neq \mathcal{E}\) when current flows.
  4. Confusing charging vs discharging RC equations. Charging: \((1 - e^{-t/\tau})\) builds up. Discharging: \(e^{-t/\tau}\) decays.
  5. Not checking units. \(RC\) must have units of seconds.
